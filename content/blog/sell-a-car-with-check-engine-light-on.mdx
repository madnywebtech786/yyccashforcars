## What a Check Engine Light Actually Means — And What It Doesn't

Let's clear something up right away because there's a lot of misunderstanding here.

A check engine light doesn't mean your car is about to explode. It doesn't mean the engine is seized or the vehicle is dangerous to be around. It means the onboard diagnostic system detected something outside of its expected parameters and logged a fault code. That's it.

What triggered that code could be something significant — a failing catalytic converter, a misfiring cylinder, a transmission issue starting to develop. But it could also be something remarkably minor — a loose gas cap, a faulty oxygen sensor that costs $40 to replace, or a sensor that picked up a temporary anomaly and never reset itself even though the actual condition was resolved weeks ago.

The light looks the same regardless of what caused it. A loose gas cap and a serious engine problem produce identical dashboard alerts. That's genuinely terrible design from a communication standpoint — but it's the reality of how these systems work.

Here's why this matters for selling your car in Calgary. A private buyer sees a check engine light and gets nervous because they don't know what it means. A dealership sees a check engine light and uses it as leverage to drop their offer significantly. But a professional cash car buyer — someone who does this every day — has seen every fault code imaginable and evaluates what's actually there rather than reacting to the warning light itself.
